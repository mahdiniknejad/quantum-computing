Quantum Dynamics, Control and Processor

Quantum Dynamics

An open quantum system is a quantum-mechanical system that interacts with an external quantum system, which is known as the bath or environment. Closed quantum systems are free from environmental effects such as leakage errors, decoherence, and quantum noises, and wise versa open systems can be affected by the environment. In the next subsections, we briefly introduce the dynamics of these two categories of quantum systems.

In this section, the Dirac notation column vector 'ket' $|\psi_t\rangle$ is called the quantum state. The conjugate-transpose ($\dagger$) of 'ket' vector which is a row vector is called 'Bra' $\langle\psi_t|=|\psi_t\rangle^{\dagger}$ in Dirac notation. By $^{T}$ and $^{\ast}$ we will show the transpose and complex conjugate of a matrix, respectively.

Closed Quantum System Dynamics: Basically, closed system dynamics describes by the well-known Schrödinger equation as:

$ \frac{d|\psi_t\rangle}{dt}=-\frac{i}{\hbar}H(t)|\psi_t\rangle $

in which $i=\sqrt{-1}$ and $\hbar$ is reduced Plank constant and we will absorb it in Hamiltonian, we set $\hbar \equiv 1$ and using the atomic unit. $H(t)$ is time dependent total Hamiltonian that can be written as the sum of quantum system internal Hamiltonian $H_0$ and control signals Hamiltonian $H_c(t)=\sum_{n=1}^{p }H_n u_n(t)$ in which $p$ is the number of control signals or equivalently the number of control Hamiltonians. The solution of Schrödinger equation is $|\psi_t\rangle=U_t|\psi_0\rangle$ in which $|\psi_0\rangle$ is the initial state and the the unitary operator is:

$ U_t=e^{-i\int_0^t H(\tau)d\tau} $

for very small time interval $dt=\delta t\rightarrow 0$, then the intermediate times are $t_k=t_0+k\delta t$,and the Hamiltonian at these intermediate times takes on the forms $\hat{H}_k=H(t_k)=H_0+\sum_{n=1}^{p} H_n u_{n}(t_k)$, then the time-evolution of quantum system at time step $k$ can be described by propagator $U_k$ decomposed as

$ U_k=\mathcal{M}_k \mathcal{M}_{k-1}...\mathcal{M}_1 \quad ,\quad \mathcal{M}_k=e^{-i\hat{H}_k\delta t} $

and the quantum state at time-step $k$ is: $|\psi_k\rangle=U_k |\psi_0\rangle$.

Von-Neumann had defined a new quantum state called density matrix as $\rho_t=|\psi_t\rangle \langle\psi_t|$, in this definition the Schrödinger equation replaced by Von-Neumann equation for a closed quantum systems including the Hamiltonian $H_S$ as:

$ \frac{d\rho_t}{dt}=-i[H_S(t),\rho_t] $

in which $[O_1,O_2]=O_1O_2-O_2O_1$ is the commutator between two quantum operators $O_1,O_2$. The solution of Von-Neumann equation for initial density matrix $\rho_0$ via aforementioned unitary operator is $\rho_{k}=U_k\rho_0 U_k^{\dagger}$. Density matrix has some properties as: $\mathrm{Tr}(\rho)=1, 0<\mathrm{Tr}(\rho^2)\leqslant 1$.

Open Quantum System Dynamics: The open quantum system in which both the measurement and the control embedded in Hamiltonian formulated by Lindblad master equation as:

$ \dot{\rho}_t=\mathcal{L}\rho_t=-i[H(t),\rho_t]+\sum_{l=1}^{q} \gamma_l\mathcal{D}[L_l]\rho_t $

in this equation, $\mathcal{L}$ is the so-called Liouvillian superoperator. The second term expresses the influence of the environment with rates $\gamma_l$ and dissipators $\mathcal{D}[L_l]\rho_t= L_l\rho_{t}L_l^{\dagger}-\frac{1}{2}(L_lL_l^{\dagger}\rho_{t}+\rho_{t}L_lL_l^{\dagger})$. Some examples of environment effect on quantum systems are relaxation, dephasing, decoherence, leakage, etc. Stochastic Schrodinger Equation (SSE) for quantum state vector is:

$ d|\psi_t\rangle=\Big(-iH(t)+\langle L^{\dagger}\rangle L-\frac{_1}{^2}L^{\dagger}L-\frac{_1}{^2}\langle L^{\dagger}\rangle\langle L\rangle\Big)|\psi_t\rangle dt+(L-\langle L\rangle)|\psi_t\rangle dW_t $

in which $\langle \mathcal{O}\rangle=\langle \psi_t|\mathcal{O}|\psi_t\rangle$.

The interaction of open quantum systems and quantum light can be formulated by quantum stochastic differential equations (QSDE) as:

$ dU_{t}=\Big(\big(-iH(t)-\frac{1}{2}LL^{\dagger}\big)dt+LdA_{t}^{\dagger}-L^{\dagger}SdA_{t} +(S-I)d\Lambda_{t} \Big) U_{t} , U_{0}=I_S\otimes I_E $

that $L$ is measurement operator, $S$ is scattering matrix, $A_{t},A_{t}^{\dagger}$ and $ \Lambda_{t}$ are annihilation, creation and Poisson quantum noises which satisfy quantum Itô rules of noise products as $dA_{t}d\Lambda_{t}=dA_{t},dA_{t}dA_{t}^\dagger=dt,d\Lambda_{t} d\Lambda_{t}=d\Lambda_{t}, d\Lambda_{t} dA_{t}^\dagger=dA_{t}^\dagger$ and the other products are equal to zero. These quantum noises can be written based on annihilation $\mathbf{a}$, creation $\mathbf{a}^{\dagger}$ and number operators $\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{a}^{\dagger}\mathbf{a}$ as $dA=\mathbf{a}dt, dA^{\dagger}=\mathbf{a}^{\dagger}dt$ and $d\Lambda=\mathbf{n}dt$. We remark that QSDE operates in the domain $S\otimes E$ and hence the multiplication between quantum systems operators and quantum noises is the tensor product.

The Hamiltonian for open quantum systems interacting with the environment is $H=H_{S}\otimes I_{E}+I_{S}\otimes H_{E}+H_I$ where $H_{S}, H_{E}, H_I$ are system, environment, and interaction Hamiltonian respectively. Quantum observable is a self-adjoin operator $X=X^\dagger$, the time evolution of this operator is $X_{t}=U_{t}^{\dagger}XU_{t}$ which the unitary operator $U_{t}$. The output quantum field operators are described by $A_{out}=U_t^{\dagger} A_t U_t , A_{out}^{\dagger}=U_t^{\dagger} A_t^{\dagger} U_t , \Lambda_{out}=U_t^{\dagger} \Lambda_t U_t$.

Also, the quantum stochastic differential equation for density matrix by quantum Itô calculus is:

$ d\rho_t=\mathcal{L}(\rho_{t})dt+[\rho_t,L^{\dagger}]dA_t+[L,\rho_t]dA^{\dagger}_t $

By non-demolition continuous measurement and quantum Itô calculus, the following quantum trajectory (filtering) can be obtained for an open quantum system:

$ d\rho_{t}=\mathcal{L}(\rho_{t})dt+\mathcal{J}_{L}(\rho_{t})dW_{t} $

that $\mathcal{J}_{L}(\rho_{t})=(L\rho_{t}+\rho_{t}L^{\dagger}-\mathrm{Tr}\big((L+L^{\dagger})\rho_t\big)\rho_{t})$ is the diffusion coefficient , also $dW_{t}=dY_{t}-\mathrm{Tr}\big((L+L^{\dagger})\rho_t\big) dt$ that $Y_{t}$ is output measurement process and $W_{t}$ is a Wiener process, $(dW_{t})^2=dt$ and $\langle X \rangle=\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_{t}X)$ is the expectation value of an quantum observable.

For photon-counting quantum jump, we will have the following stochastic differential equation:

$ d\rho_t=\mathcal{J}_{(-iH-\frac{1}{2}L^{\dagger}L)}(\rho_t)dt+g_{L}(\rho_t)dN_t \; , \; g_{L}(\rho_t)=\frac{L\rho_tL^{\dagger}}{\mathrm{Tr}\big(L\rho_tL^{\dagger}\big)}-\rho_t $

in which $N_t$ is photon counting with statistic $E(dN_t)=\langle L^{\dagger}L\rangle dt$.

Quantum Control Systems

There are several well-known control design methods for quantum systems such as nonlinear Lyapunov control, optimal control methods and recently learning-based quantum control methods.

In Lyapunov control, we must find a positive definite energy-like function of quantum state or unitary operator and design the control vector in a way that the time derivative of this function is negative definite, in this situation we have the stability grantee for convergence (in a defined norm) to desired state or desired unitary matrix. As an example if we consider the dynamics of quantum system be described by state vector $\psi_t$ and the desired state to be converge to be $\psi_d$, then we can have several Lyapunov function candidates such as function of state vectors distance $V=\frac{1}{2}\big(1-|\langle \psi_d|\psi_t \rangle|^2 \big)$ or the function of state vectors similarity as $V=\frac{1}{2}\langle \psi_t-\psi_d | \psi_t-\psi_d \rangle$. Another example of quantum dynamics described by a density-matrix can be as $V=\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_d^2)-\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_t\rho_d)$ and in this case a Lyapunov control vector can be as:

$ u_n=-i\alpha_n\mathrm{Tr}([H_n,\rho_t]\rho_d) , \alpha_n >0 $. As another sample, suppose we want to realize a quantum unitary gate $\mathcal{O}$, then the Lyapunov function is: $V(t)=1-\frac{1}{N^2}|\mathrm{Tr}(\mathcal{O}^{\dagger} U_t)|^2$, then the Lyapunov stabilizing control signal is ($H=H_0+u(t)H_c$): $u(t)=-\alpha \mathcal{R}e\Big(\mathrm{Tr}(-i\mathcal{O}^{\dagger}H_c U_t) \mathrm{Tr}(\mathcal{O}^T U_t^{\ast})\Big)$.

For optimal quantum control, an objective function must be defined and optimal control must be designed in such a way that minimizes the objective function. The control method can be based on Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) formulation, bang-bang optimal control, gradient ascent pulse engineering (GRAPE), the Krotov method, or any other optimal controls. However, until l now, the convergence of optimal quantum control method is not proved analytically, but there is a fact in practice that optimal control methods both have a faster convergence rate and better performance.

Quantum Processor

To implement quantum algorithms, a set of unitary gates must be realized with high fidelity with every possible longer coherence time. Quantum algorithms can be developed to solve intractable problems and their applications, the realization of gate-based of quantum algorithms, derivation of suitable high-fidelity long coherence time quantum control strategy for implementation of quantum gates of quantum circuits of quantum algorithms, readouts, quantum interconnection issues, and many problems encounter for real physical implementations and generally a quantum operating systems (QOS) to carry out user tasks both classical and quantum tasks.

QuCTech aims to devise a quantum-computer (Instruction set) architecture with such a powerful and optimized design that can be used for making commercial quantum processors. Among different building blocks of quantum processor instruction set architecture, quantum barrel shifter, quantum bus matrix, quantum arithmetic logic unit (QALU), quantum processing unit (QPU), quantum control unit (QCU), quantum language programming, quantum random number generator (QRNG), Quantum-GPU, Quantum-RAM(QRAM) have significant priority. Several other quantum designs such as quantum neural network engine(QNNE), quantum fetch unit, quantum pipeline, quantum execute unit, quantum decoding are other quantum computing hardware essentials to be addressed for quantum processor design. All designs first will be carried out at the quantum logic gate level and then in the physical device layer for optimization, control, and in near future the fabrication.
